A JOYOUS WEEK IN MY YOGA LIFE
WITH TWO BELOVED FRIENDS
by
BARBARA DE JONG

In 1970, having been attending Yoga classes in Pretoria with Malsie Hamman and her daughter Janice, I'd had the good fortune to be offered the chance to fill a last-minute vacancy in the 20-strong South African group travelling to Mauritius to study with B.K.S. Iyengar – even though I was a relative newcomer to Yoga. At that time South African passport-holders were unwelcome in India, and, as the Indian government refused to grant permission for Iyengar to travel to South Africa, it was necessary for neutral ground, Mauritius in this instance, to be found if our students were to receive tuition from him. That was how I met Joyce Stuart (Padma), Iyengar's first student from South Africa, and so it was logical that in 1973 when my family relocated to Howick in the then Natal – a great upheaval with six young children – I should join Padma's active Yoga group in Pietermaritzburg.

By 1976 a Southern African branch of the B.K.S. Iyengar Yoga Institute was formed, headquarters at first in Pietermaritzburg, and Padma and I alternated as Chair and Vice-Chair of the Natal group (later Region) for many years.

In 1979 the Indian government finally relented: Iyengar was granted permission to come to South Africa. One of the objects of his visit that year was to inaugurate and give his blessing to a building in the grounds of the home of Padma and her husband Ron Stuart, a building patterned (with Iyengar's permission) on the asana room of the Ramamani Iyengar Memorial Yoga Institute in Pune, India, which was to bear his name and be dedicated to the study and practice of Iyengar Yoga.

After the inauguration – a dream come true for Padma and Ron – a tour of the country by road was scheduled. The evening before we set off Guruji came to stay at my family's Howick ranch, where he was to spend the night in a guest cottage. Before we retired after dinner, all of us, including Guruji, my husband Gerrie and I, as well as the children, gathered in the study. I should note that Gerrie, although he'd always encouraged me in my Yoga practice, had never tried it himself, even though, like many tall men (6'5”) he had back problems. He was unaware of the imbalances in his body relating to his posture. Which imbalances were, of course, very evident to our guest. Some of our children still speak of the time their father was literally charmed by a small Indian man into lying on his back on our large square coffee table, head hanging off one side, arms extended at the side of his head, trunk supported by the table, and knees bent with feet firmly on the floor. No one was more surprised than Gerrie himself, and the room filled with delighted laughter from everyone present, including Guruji.

The next morning Guruji, Padma and I set off on the first stage of our road trip, which was, at Guruji's request, to include the famous Pretoria Zoo and the Big Hole at Kimberley, as well as the Western Cape.

Younger readers need to know that in those days – 36 years ago – two white women travelling by car in the company of an Indian man in South Africa was unusual, to say the least, and demanded some careful planning to ensure that our guest was shielded from what could have been racially embarrassing (or deeply upsetting) situations. At each overnight stop I would leave the other two in my car and, entering the hotel, ask for the manager to explain that we needed a large room or suite (space for his personal practice) for an Indian man, and a smaller twin-bedded room for two women accompanying him. Even stops for meals required a preamble to ensure that it was acceptable for us to sit together in the dining room without offending management or other guests.

From the beginning of our journey Guruji found the use of a seat belt restrictive and asked not to be required to “buckle up”. In those days, though it was compulsory for front-seat passengers, the belt was optional for those at the back. However, so eager was Guruji to miss nothing of the landscapes through which we passed that he soon gave up the freedom and comfort of the back seat.

The morning at the Pretoria Zoo was one of the most memorable. There were the chimpanzees doing their version of Adho Mukha Svanasana, Dandasana, Halasana, Baddha Konasana and other Yoga poses, swinging effortlessly back and forth from one to the next, using ropes in ways that even B.K.S. Iyengar had never imagined. Padma and I loved seeing Guruji's delight in this, and also loved hearing him later berate the orangutans for not getting their postures exactly right. What a marvellously happy morning that was.

Next stop was Kimberley. The museum outside the city was fascinating: on display were samples of diamond matrix, as well as charts and historical facts concerning the size and chronology of the more famous diamonds. At the De Beers House Museum we saw also details and illustrations of the sorting and sizing of the stones (at that time cut and polished in Holland and then returned to South Africa), and Guruji was intrigued by two facts: first, that diamond is the hardest substance on earth and can only be cut by diamond itself, and second, that diamonds have perfect cleavage, which means that the crystal structure allows extremely smooth fractures to occur when force is applied in appropriate directions. In lecture-demonstrations Guruji gave some years later in both Cape Town and Johannesburg, he referred to these unique characteristics and compared them to the human body when attempting Yoga poses.

The Big Hole impresses everyone approaching by air or by road. The sheer size of the largest open-pit mine in the world, all dug out by hand, is staggering. Certainly, standing at the edge of the crater each of us felt our own insignificance and each, in our own different way, reflected on the meaning of life and on how the three of us had come together to this place at this time. For we were indeed disparate in our origins: myself, a geologist, wife and mother, born and raised in America; Padma, born and educated in Britain before, in her teens, coming to South Africa with her family and settling in Pietermaritzburg; and an Indian man already world-renowned as a foremost exponent of Hatha Yoga. This Yoga was the thread that had so drawn us together in a car driving through South Africa in 1979. 

The first sight of Cape Town was, as always, awe-inspiring, with the mountain outlined against the sky on a beautiful sunny day. At that time my husband was a Member of Parliament, and we were able to spend the first morning in the Parliament building. In the entrance hall was a great Book of Remembrance commemorating those in the South African armed forces who had died in the World Wars: a page of the book was ceremoniously turned every day that Parliament was in session. To our astonishment it was open at a page on which was listed the name of Padma's brother, Kenneth Wilson Prescott, who had been killed in action in World War 2. This was particularly striking and very moving for Padma, who had never previously been to Parliament and was not even aware of the existence of the book.

After this B.K.S. Iyengar's journey was put into the hands of the Western Cape members of the Institute, and Padma and I travelled home without him – but with wonderful memories of time spent with a remarkable man, not just our revered teacher, but also now our friend.

To have watched Guruji work on Gerrie's uneven spine, to have witnessed his joy at the “yogic” antics of the great apes, to have seen the characteristics of diamonds from his unique point of view, to have shared with him and with Padma the emotional experience in the Parliament building, was all so stirring, so very special, that it seemed to have a spiritual dimension, for me at least. In retrospect, certainly, I feel that it contributed to my own spiritual development. I believe the effect of Guruji on my life, from the work I did under his tutelage during a number of courses, through my attempts to interpret and transmit this to my students, and on to the shining example of pure dedication he's been to me and to so many, has been of immeasurable benefit and importance to me. I have been able to accept with reasonable grace the daily challenges I've had to face since the diagnosis of Motor Neuron Disease (ALS) 15 years ago. I'm still able to lead a happy, fairly independent life, though wheelchair-bound, adapting my personal Yoga practice to what is possible each day, and finding a degree of contentment in whatever that is, without looking back to what might have been. I am profoundly, inexpressibly, grateful.
